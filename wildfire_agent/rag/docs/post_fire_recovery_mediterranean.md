---
title: "Post-Fire Vegetation Recovery in Mediterranean Ecosystems"
location: "Mediterranean Basin"
year: 2023
type: ecology
source: "European Journal of Forest Research; EFFIS Post-Fire Assessment Guidelines; ESA Copernicus Land Service; Pausas & Keeley (2009) Trends in Ecology & Evolution; Moreira et al. (2020) Annual Review of Ecology"
---

Mediterranean ecosystems have co-evolved with fire over millennia, and many plant communities exhibit specific fire-adaptation traits. However, the rate and quality of recovery depend heavily on fire severity, post-fire climate conditions, and the interval since the previous fire.

**Resprouting and seeding strategies**: Dominant recovery mechanisms in Mediterranean shrublands (maquis, phrygana, garrigue) are resprouting from protected root crowns or lignotubers (Cistus, Quercus coccifera, Arbutus unedo) and obligate seeding triggered by heat or smoke (Cistus spp., Pinus brutia). Recovery of shrub cover can begin within weeks post-fire and reach 50–70% canopy closure within 3–5 years under average rainfall conditions.

**Forest recovery timelines**: Closed-canopy pine forests (Pinus halepensis, P. brutia) can regenerate from serotinous or semi-serotinous cones within 2–5 years if seed sources survived at fire margins. Fir forests (Abies cephalonica) on Greek mountains recover more slowly — 20–40 years for closed canopy. Old-growth oak woodlands (Quercus petraea, Q. pubescens) may require 50–100 years to restore structural complexity and associated biodiversity.

**dNBR as recovery predictor**: High-severity burn scars (dNBR > 0.44) are associated with significantly slower recovery trajectories due to near-complete loss of soil seed banks, root systems, and organic matter. Time-series NBR analysis (annual Sentinel-2 composites) is used by Copernicus to track recovery trajectories at landscape scale.

**Erosion risk**: The first 1–3 post-fire winters carry high erosion and flash flood risk, particularly on steep slopes. Post-fire stabilization interventions (straw mulch, contour felling, seeding) are recommended in ESA and EU civil protection guidelines for slopes exceeding 30%.

**Climate feedback**: Increased fire frequency under climate change may push some Mediterranean communities below minimum inter-fire interval for recovery, leading to type conversion from forest to persistent shrubland or grassland.
