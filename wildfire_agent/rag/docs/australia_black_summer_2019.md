title: "2019–2020 Australian Black Summer Bushfires"
location: "Eastern Australia (New South Wales, Victoria, Queensland, South Australia)"

source: "Royal Commission into National Natural Disaster Arrangements (Commonwealth of Australia, 2020); Australian Bureau of Meteorology; CSIRO; NASA FIRMS; Copernicus Atmosphere Monitoring Service (CAMS)"
---

The 2019–2020 Australian bushfire season, known as the "Black Summer," burned from approximately June 2019 through March 2020 across southeastern Australia. It was the most destructive fire season in Australian recorded history by burned area, economic loss, biodiversity impact, and international atmospheric visibility.

An estimated 18.6 million ha burned across Australia during the season, with approximately 12.6 million ha in New South Wales alone. Over 3,000 homes were destroyed and at least 33 people died directly from the fires; hundreds more deaths were attributed to smoke inhalation across eastern cities. An estimated 3 billion animals were killed or displaced, representing one of the largest acute wildlife mortality events ever recorded.

Smoke from the Black Summer fires was detected by Copernicus CAMS satellites circling the globe and reaching South America. The fires generated multiple pyrocumulonimbus (pyroCb) events — fire-generated thunderstorms that injected smoke into the stratosphere, affecting global atmospheric chemistry.

The 2019–2020 season followed Australia's hottest and driest year on record (2019), with the Indian Ocean Dipole in a strongly positive phase reducing rainfall across southeastern Australia.

The Royal Commission into National Natural Disaster Arrangements (2020) identified systemic gaps in Commonwealth-state coordination, early warning systems, and the capacity to scale up firefighting resources. It recommended a permanent national aerial firefighting fleet and improved interoperability between state services.

The Black Summer is a benchmark event in global wildfire science, cited in IPCC AR6 as evidence of climate change increasing fire weather frequency and severity.
