Dadia National Park (formally Dadia–Lefkimi–Soufli Forest National Park) covers approximately 43,000 ha in the Evros regional unit of northeastern Greece, designated as Natura 2000 site GR1110006. The park is one of the most ecologically significant areas in Europe for large raptors and is recognized as an Important Bird Area (IBA) by BirdLife International.

The park's forests are dominated by a mosaic of sessile oak (Quercus petraea), Valonia oak (Q. ithaburensis ssp. macrolepis), black pine (Pinus nigra), and Calabrian pine (P. brutia). These old-growth oak-pine stands provide critical nesting and roosting habitat for species that require large, undisturbed forested landscapes.

The most globally significant feature of Dadia is its breeding colony of the Black Vulture (Aegypius monachus, Cinereous Vulture), classified as Vulnerable on the IUCN Red List. Dadia hosts one of the largest Black Vulture breeding populations in Europe, with approximately 30–40 active nests counted in recent surveys before the 2023 fire. The park also supports significant populations of White-tailed Eagle (Haliaeetus albicilla), Short-toed Eagle (Circaetus gallicus), Imperial Eagle (Aquila heliaca), and Long-legged Buzzard (Buteo rufinus).

The 2023 Dadia wildfire burned through extensive areas of the park's core zone, destroying primary forested habitat used by Black Vultures and other raptors for nesting. Post-fire surveys by the Hellenic Ornithological Society documented displacement of vulture colonies and loss of established nest trees. Recovery of old-growth oak forest at Dadia is expected to require 50–100 years, posing a severe long-term threat to the Black Vulture colony's viability.
