The Mediterranean climate zone (hot dry summers, mild wet winters) creates a predictable seasonal fire regime. The combination of summer drought, senescent vegetation, and persistent dry winds produces recurring high fire-danger periods typically lasting June through September in southern Europe.

**Key meteorological drivers**: Fire weather in the Mediterranean is primarily driven by three interacting factors:
1. *Temperature and drought*: summer maxima above 35–40°C accelerate fuel drying; multi-week dry spells reduce live and dead fuel moisture to critical levels
2. *Wind*: persistent regional winds — the Etesian (Meltemi) in the Aegean, the Tramontane and Mistral in France, the Ponente in Spain — promote rapid fire spread and suppress aerial firefighting
3. *Relative humidity*: values below 20–30% at critical thresholds for flashy fire behavior

**Etesian winds and Greece**: The Etesian (Meltemi) wind system dominates the Aegean from June through September, with northerly or north-easterly flows reaching sustained speeds of 40–60 km/h. The 2023 Dadia fire, the 2021 Evia fire, and the 2007 Peloponnese fires all occurred during Etesian episodes.

**Lightning ignition vs. human ignition**: In Mediterranean Europe, human ignition (accidental, arson, agricultural burning) accounts for over 90% of documented fire starts. Lightning ignition is more common in northern Mediterranean mountains and Iberia.

**Fire return intervals**: Natural or quasi-natural fire return intervals in Mediterranean pine and shrubland ecosystems are estimated at 15–50 years. Abandonment of rural land since the 1970s has dramatically increased fuel loads, reducing effective fire return intervals and increasing the potential for high-severity fire behavior when ignition occurs.
