title: "Climate Change and Wildfire Risk in Greece"
location: "Greece / Eastern Mediterranean"
year: 2023
type: science
source: "IPCC AR6 WGI Chapter 12 (2021); Turco et al. (2017) Nature Climate Change; Greek National Climate Change Adaptation Plan (2016); Copernicus Climate Change Service (C3S) regional assessments; JRC PESETA IV report"
---

Greece and the broader eastern Mediterranean are classified as a climate change hotspot — one of the regions projected to experience the most severe warming and drying trends globally. Multiple lines of evidence indicate that fire weather conditions in Greece are becoming more frequent and more intense.

**Observed trends**: The eastern Mediterranean has warmed approximately 1.5°C above pre-industrial levels since 1900, roughly 20% faster than the global mean. Summer precipitation has declined in many regions. EFFIS data show a trend toward larger individual fire events and longer fire seasons in Greece since the 1980s, with several record-breaking seasons occurring after 2000 (2007, 2021, 2023).

**Projected changes under RCP 4.5 and 8.5**: JRC PESETA IV and Copernicus C3S assessments project that:
- The number of days with extreme fire weather (FWI above 30) in Greece will increase by 30–70% by mid-century under intermediate emissions scenarios
- The fire season length will extend by 3–6 weeks in southern Greece
- Drought frequency will increase, with multi-year drought sequences becoming more common

**Compound events**: The intersection of heat waves, droughts, and Etesian wind episodes — the combination that drove the 2023 Evros, 2021 Evia, and 2007 Peloponnese fires — is projected to become significantly more frequent. Multi-region simultaneous fires, which overwhelmed response capacity in 2023, are a particular concern.

**Land use interaction**: Climate-driven fire risk amplifies on landscapes shaped by rural depopulation and land abandonment. Fuel accumulation on abandoned agricultural terraces and former pastures throughout Greece has increased the potential for high-intensity fire spread when fire-weather thresholds are exceeded.
